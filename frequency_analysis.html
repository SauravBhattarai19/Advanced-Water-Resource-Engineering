What is a Probability Density Function (PDF)?

📊 The Histogram's Limitation

  • • Depends on bin size choice
  • • Blocky and discontinuous
  • • Can't find probability of exact values
  • • **Can't extrapolate** beyond observed data

📈 The PDF Solution

  • • Smooth, continuous mathematical curve
  • • Models the "ideal" shape of the data
  • • Can predict probabilities anywhere
  • • **Allows extrapolation** to rare events

Key Concept: PDF Height = Probability Density

A PDF doesn't give probabilities directly. Instead, the **area under the curve** between any two points gives the probability that a value falls in that range.

The Normal Distribution: Nature's Bell Curve

📊 Key Characteristics

  • • **Perfectly symmetrical** bell shape
  • • Mean = Median = Mode
  • • Defined by just 2 parameters: μ (mean) and σ (std dev)
  • • 68% of data within 1σ, 95% within 2σ

✅ Works Great For:

  • • Human heights, weights
  • • Test scores (with enough data)
  • • Measurement errors
  • • Sum of many random effects

❌ Poor Choice For:

  • • Data that can't be negative (incomes, sizes)
  • • Skewed data (most values on one side)
  • • **Extreme values (like flood peaks)**

Parameters:

Mean (μ) = 175 cm

Std Dev (σ) = 10 cm

The Gumbel Distribution: Built for Extremes

📊 Key Characteristics

  • • **Right-skewed** (tail extends right)
  • • Part of Extreme Value Theory
  • • Parameters: μ (location) and β (scale)
  • • Mode is to the left of mean

✅ Perfect For:

  • • Annual maximum temperatures
  • • **Annual peak floods**
  • • Highest wind speeds per year
  • • Any "maximum of many values"

🎯 Why It Works:

  • • Mathematically derived for extremes
  • • Has natural right skew
  • • Gives proper weight to rare, large events

🧮 Example: Annual Maximum Daily Temperature

Parameters:

Location (μ) = 35°C

Scale (β) = 3°C

At mode (33.3°C):

PDF = 0.123

(most likely extreme temp)

P(temp > 40°C):

≈ 5% chance each year

Right tail probability

The Log-Normal Distribution: When Growth Multiplies

📊 Key Characteristics

  • • **Right-skewed**, can only be positive
  • • If log(X) is Normal, then X is Log-Normal
  • • Parameters: μ and σ of the logarithm
  • • Long right tail, short left tail

✅ Great For:

  • • Personal incomes
  • • City population sizes
  • • Stock prices
  • • **Some flood data (related to LP3)**

🎯 The Key Insight:

  • • Results from multiplicative processes
  • • Many small % changes compound
  • • Natural for positive-only quantities

From PDF to CDF: The Cumulative Distribution Function

📊 PDF (Probability Density Function)

  • What it shows: The "shape" of the distribution
  • Y-axis: Probability density (not probability!)
  • Usage: Area under curve = probability of range
  • Analogy: Like the speed at each moment of a car trip

📈 CDF (Cumulative Distribution Function)

  • What it shows: P(Flow ≤ x)
  • Y-axis: Actual probability (0 to 1)
  • Usage: Direct probability reading
  • Analogy: Like total distance traveled by a certain time

For Engineering: We want the **Exceedance Probability**

P(Flow > x) = 1 - CDF(x)

This tells us the probability that flow **exceeds** a certain value - exactly what we need for design!

Final Step: From Probability to Risk

A "100-year flood" has a 1% chance of happening *this year*. But what's the chance it will happen over the 50-year life of our bridge? This is the **Hydrologic Risk**.

Hydrologic Risk Calculator

Risk is the probability that one or more events of a given magnitude will occur within a specified period.

$$ R = 1 - (1 - P)^n = 1 - (1 - \frac{1}{T})^n $$

Risk of failure during the project's life:

39.5%

Notice how a seemingly small 1% annual probability results in a very significant ~40% risk over a typical project lifetime!
